Our Venues

The Theatre Royal operates within The Hedberg, a world-class performing arts precinct in the heart of Hobart, shared with the University of Tasmania.

🎭

Theatre Royal

700 seats

Australia's oldest continuously operating theatre, open since 1837. Built with convict-carved stone, the Louis Quatorze-style auditorium features iconic red velvet, gilded highlights and a crystal chandelier in a domed ceiling. Noel Coward called it "a dream of a theatre."

🎬

Studio Theatre

Up to 300 seats

A state-of-the-art flexible performance space within The Hedberg. Purpose-built for contemporary performance formats, it's intimate, adaptable, and perfect for mid-scale productions, experimental work, and touring shows.

🎵

Ian Potter Recital Hall

Concert hall

A world-class acoustic venue forming part of The Hedberg's music precinct, shared with UTAS Conservatorium. Designed for chamber music, recitals, and amplified performances with exceptional acoustic treatment.

🌇

Vanessa Goodwin City Room

50 people

Connected and versatile, fully reconfigurable, enabling creative collaboration, corporate meetings, and community events. The adjoining Vanessa Goodwin Rooftop Garden provides an alfresco breakout space with sweeping views over Nipaluna/Hobart.

🎷

The Salon

Intimate

Inviting and perfectly formed. Featuring many of the same acoustic capabilities as the Ian Potter Recital Hall, The Salon has two sound and lighting states and flexible audience positions. A conceptual, prototyping workshop space for students, industry and community.

Our History

Australia's oldest continuously operating theatre, spanning nearly 190 years of drama, passion, demolition, and survival.

1834

A Colony Builds a Theatre

In April 1834, amid voices declaring Hobart too small and too poor to justify a theatre, a consortium of Hobart Town's leading citizens met to make one happen. Peter Degraves, founder of the Cascade Brewery, trained engineer, and shipbuilder, emerged as the driving force, proposing a design and ultimately funding the project himself when the economic downturn of the late 1830s caused most investors to pull out.

Colonial architect John Lee Archer approved the design: a provincial Georgian masterpiece. The site chosen was a patch of dockside land in Wapping, a rough neighbourhood of pubs, brothels, tanneries, and the refuse-choked Hobart Rivulet.

The laying of the foundation stone on 4 November 1834 was a spectacular event. The Colonial Times reported over a thousand people present, ships in the harbour flying their colours, and guns firing salutes as the band played God Save the King.

1837

Opening Night, and the Shades Below

The theatre held its first public event before it was even finished, a retirement gathering for Lieutenant Governor Colonel Kenneth Snodgrass in January 1837. The official opening came in March, with performances of Speed the Plough and The Spoiled Child, lit by whale oil lamps.

Degraves had designed three entrances to serve Hobart's strictly stratified society: a grand front entrance for the gentry, a side lane door for working families, and a third through a pub built directly into the theatre's basement, housing The Shades, which opened straight into the cheapest pit seats. In June 1837, the theatre was renamed the Royal Victoria Theatre to celebrate the coronation of Queen Victoria.

1856–1900

Renovations, Cockfights & Changing Fashions

Degraves undertook the first major renovation in 1856, adding a gallery to increase capacity. Throughout this period the theatre hosted almost as many cockfights, boxing matches, religious rallies, and political meetings as plays and concerts. It was a venue of the people, for better or worse.

On Degraves' death in 1883, the theatre was sold to Richard Lewis for £3,222. Lewis spent the next four decades remodelling and upgrading the building to meet changing fashions, rebuilding the stage, extending it, and adding an orchestra pit.

1911

The Auditorium We Know Today

The most extensive renovation in the theatre's history began in 1911. Melbourne theatre architect William Pitt created the Louis Quatorze style auditorium that survives to this day. The Shades tavern was replaced with raked stalls seating. A steeper, higher balcony was built. For the first time, plush red velvet upholstery was installed throughout, along with paintings and a magnificent crystal chandelier hanging from the domed ceiling.

Noel Coward, who performed here, described it simply as "a dream of a theatre." Vivien Leigh and Laurence Olivier also graced the stage in this era.

1940s–50s

Saved From Demolition

The theatre's darkest hour came in the late 1940s. With live theatre in decline and plans emerging to demolish the building for a transport hub, the Theatre Royal faced extinction. It was Laurence Olivier himself who launched a national appeal for its preservation.

The real saviours on the ground were Gwen Friend and her partner Fifi Banvard, who relocated from Sydney, using Gwen's own private funds to refurbish the theatre and establish a permanent theatre company. Between July 1950 and March 1951 they staged a new production every two weeks, an extraordinary feat of commitment that secured the theatre's future.

1984

Fire

A devastating fire gutted the interior in 1984, destroying much of what remained of the Victorian fitout. Rather than signal the end, it prompted a major restoration effort, painstakingly recreating the Louis Quatorze decorative scheme, the red velvet seating, and the crystal chandelier. The theatre that exists today is the product of that restoration.

Today

The Hedberg, History Meets the Future

In recent years the Theatre Royal has been joined by The Hedberg, a world-class contemporary performing arts precinct built around and in celebration of the original Georgian building. Shared with the University of Tasmania's Conservatorium of Music, The Hedberg adds the Studio Theatre, the Ian Potter Recital Hall, world-class production facilities, and a striking new public face to what was once a rough Wapping back street.

The result is unique: a 187-year-old convict-built theatre nestled inside a 21st-century arts centre, still staging performances, still welcoming artists from around the world, and still, somehow, the same theatre that opened by whale oil lamplight in March 1837.

👻
Fred the Ghost

No history of the Theatre Royal would be complete without Fred, the theatre's resident ghost. His origins are disputed: some say he was a stagehand who fell to his death from the fly tower in the early 20th century; others believe he was a sailor murdered in The Shades, the rough tavern built into the theatre's basement. Whatever the truth, Fred has been a permanent presence ever since. Staff and performers report cold spots, moving objects, and unexplained footsteps backstage to this day. He remains, by all accounts, the theatre's longest-running cast member.

Tunapri Kani

The Acknowledgement Project

Prior to every performance in our venues, you will hear an Acknowledgement of Country from Uncle Jim Everett (puralia meenamatta).

Acknowledgement of Country

Recognising Nipaluna

By sharing an Acknowledgement of Country, the Theatre Royal recognises that we live and share culture in Nipaluna on the traditional lands of the Muwinina people. We respect the millennia of storytelling, music and dance that has taken place on this land, pay respect to the Elders that have come before, and the continuing custodianship of the Palawa peoples of Lutruwita/Tasmania.

Elder in Residence

Uncle Jim Everett (puralia meenamatta)

With a significant legacy in the development of Tasmanian arts and culture, Uncle Jim is a pioneering and unique voice, representing the stories of the Tasmanian Aboriginal community for over five decades. A poet, playwright, activist and Tasmanian Aboriginal Elder, we honour Uncle Jim's enormous legacy to arts and culture.

About the Recording

Hedberg Recording Studio, 18 February 2022

With text created by Sinsa Mansell, the recording was made with Uncle Jim and Sinsa in the Hedberg Recording Studio. It was first played on 21 October 2022, on the opening night of tuylupa, a dance work by pakana kanaplila and Soma Lumia, celebrating Uncle Jim's 80th birthday.

Two recordings were made for use when there is no live Acknowledgement or Welcome to Country, using both English and Palawa kani, the language of Tasmanian Aboriginals. One version is used for performances with First Peoples artists or stories; a second for all other productions.

Project Legacy

Supporting Future Generations

Every time we play an Acknowledgement, the Theatre Royal pays a royalty to a fund that supports development opportunities for Tasmanian Aboriginal artists. This project is a lasting legacy for future generations.

🎭 What This Means for Your Show

At the beginning of your show, we kindly request that the house lights are dimmed by half for 70 seconds while the Acknowledgement is played to the audience.

Our front-of-house staff will maintain a lockout on the doors during this time, ensuring no audience members enter. No action is required from your team. We'll take care of everything.
